Lesson Number: 52 - Subtracting Numbers with More Than Three Digits & Word Problems

Problem 1
There were 8 buses. Each bus could seat 60 students. How many students could ride in all the buses?

Answer: 480 students

Explanation: This is a multiplication problem. We have 8 groups (buses) of 60 items (students).

$$ 60 \times 8 = 480 $$
Problem 2
Each van could carry 9 students. There were 63 students. How many vans were needed to carry all of the students?

Answer: 7 vans

Explanation: This is a division problem. We are dividing the total students (63) by the capacity of each van (9).

$$ 63 \div 9 = 7 $$
Problem 3
The coach separated 28 players into 4 equal teams. How many players were on each team?

Answer: 7 players

Explanation: Divide the total players (28) by the number of teams (4).

$$ 28 \div 4 = 7 $$
Problem 4
There are 10 swimmers in the race. Only 3 can be awarded medals. How many swimmers will not win a medal?

Answer: 7 swimmers

Explanation: Subtract the winners from the total number of swimmers.

$$ 10 - 3 = 7 $$

Problem 27
If \( \triangle = \square \), which of these is not necessarily true?
A. \( \triangle + 2 = \square + 2 \)
B. \( 2 \times \triangle = 2 \times \square \)
C. \( \triangle - 2 = \square - 2 \)
D. \( 2 \times \triangle = \square + 2 \)

Answer: D

Explanation: Since the triangle and square are equal, any operation done to one side must be done to the other to maintain equality.

  • A adds 2 to both. (True)
  • B multiplies both by 2. (True)
  • C subtracts 2 from both. (True)
  • D multiplies one by 2 and adds 2 to the other. This is only true if \( \triangle = 2 \). It is not necessarily true for all numbers.

Problem 29
The answer to 33 divided by 8 is not a whole number. What whole number represents a reasonable estimate of the answer? Explain why you chose that number.

Answer: 4

Explanation: Multiples of 8 are 8, 16, 24, 32, 40. The number 33 is very close to 32. Since \( 32 \div 8 = 4 \), 4 is the best whole number estimate.

Problem 30
Look at these coins. List all of the different amounts you could make using exactly two coins. Arrange the amounts in order from least to greatest and write each amount with a dollar sign.
(Coins shown: Quarter, Quarter, Penny, Half Dollar)

Answer: $0.26, $0.50, $0.51, $0.75

Explanation: The coins are 2 Quarters ($0.25), 1 Penny ($0.01), and 1 Half Dollar ($0.50). We need to find sums of exactly two coins:

  • Quarter + Penny = $0.25 + $0.01 = $0.26
  • Quarter + Quarter = $0.25 + $0.25 = $0.50
  • Half Dollar + Penny = $0.50 + $0.01 = $0.51
  • Half Dollar + Quarter = $0.50 + $0.25 = $0.75
